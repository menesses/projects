Scanning Imager

In the Cira Lab, we needed the ability to scan over a 30cm X 30cm area and take images of predetermied locations. We also wanted the ability to use epifluorescence for some image sets. I designed a stage-mounted imaging system to accomplish this task. The stage, purchased as a kit from OpenBuilds, was belt driven and controlled using a GRBL controller. The optical system featured two swappable lenses which enabled a wide range of magnifications (our setup spanned 0.5x-4.4x). A collimation arm allowed simple epi-illumination, and filtersets were easily incorporated within a filter cube located between the two lenses. Some images of the system, and some models are featured to the right. The MATLAB code can be found here. A more formal write-up on this system is coming soon!

    • Jet Drops Education Activity

    Working with the Museum of Science, I helped create a hands-on activity teaching the public about jet drop production and aerosol generation from bubbles. The activity sought to engage both young museum go-ers as well as their caregivers. First pouring soda water into a small cup, we asked the participants to make observations and draw distinctions between a bubble and a drop. Adding a small amount of sugar into the cup results in a flurry of bubbles as they nucleate on the sugar crystals. As we do this, the participants hold a clear slide above the cup, which quickly becomes coated in tiny droplets. We ask the participants to brainstorm how droplets might be generated by these bubbles before revealing the videos (included to the left.) We have them observe that the smaller of the two bubbles generates a small droplet that is launched much higher and faster than the larger bubble.

    We ran this activity annually between 2015-2019 at the Museum of Science, generally with good reception. The everyday materials used helps to ensure this is an activity that can be replicated and performed in a variety of settings. It helps to draw connections between the effects of jet drops in everyday life (i.e. the "fizz" in soda, the smell of the sea).

    Dog-cam

    When we first got our dog, Sadie, we had some hesitancy leaving her home alone, so I decided this was a good chance to play around with a Raspberry Pi. The goal was to have a camera that I could mount and pan to get maximum visual coverage. I went with an SG90 servo for panning as I had one on-hand, and I used a Raspberry Pi Zero W with the Raspberry Pi Camera V2 for video. The Pi Zero ran a Python Flask server which served up the video feed. I had the camera pivot to discrete locations using the Python script to fire up the servo when a particular page was fetched (i.e. foo.bar/couch, foo.bar/door, foo.bar/hallway). To access the video feed while out and about, I setup the Pi Zero with a dynamic DNS through noip.com When mounted in a doorway to our living room, we had good coverage of the typical places Sadie would be.

    All-in-all, this project worked pretty great as far as a week long hack goes. As you can see from the pictures, the early version was litterally held together by cardboard and duct tape. Nevertheless, we were able to use the camera while we were out of the house to get some reassurance that Sadie wasn't too anxious. We stopped using the camera before I had a chance to improve the mounting, but I've included some designs I considered printing. Perhaps using a beefier RPi, it could be possible to do object tracking and follow Sadie (if she ever got off the couch). This was my first time using Flask and the RPi Cam, so the code is not pretty. I may clean it up and post it here sometime in the future.
